Supervised by Professor William Schuler, I participated in two research projects about surprisal, one with data from eye-tracking and self-paced reading corpora and another with a computational cognitive model and hand-crafted sentences and grammar. ▶ Project #1

I used and modified the code from the psycholinguistic toolkit modelblocks to process and conduct statistical analysis on the data from PROVO and GECO, two eye-tracking corpora. The analysis was then used to prove that as language models increase in size and the amount of training data, they perform worse in predicting surprisal as their superhuman accuracy prediction of rare words. The work is under review at European Chapter of the Association for Computational Linguistics (EACL).

▶ Project #2

I employed and refactored a left corner parser built on distributed associative memory, a model first used in Rasmussen and Schuler (2018). The memory and cues are stored in vectors and the associations between them are represented as matrices. The model demonstrates the parallel propagation of different analyses and the correlation between predictability and surprisal, which are proved with a set of hand-crafted sentences and grammar. We are now compiling the results for a paper aimed at Cognitive Science.
